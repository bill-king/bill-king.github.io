Beginnings

In October 2014, Walter Wall and Bill King backpacked a challenging 25 miles from Wawona to Savage’s Trading Post via the notorious South Fork Trail to witness first-hand the lore of poison oak, heat, rattlesnakes and forgotten trails. What we discovered was a canyon rich in wildness, full of solitude, and marked with alluring mystery. It was also evident that the path would soon fade away like many others. At the time, Walt and Bill were members of the Mariposa County Search and Rescue Team, and believed the trail should be lightly maintained for the safety of future hikers. The South Fork Trailblazer volunteer group was formed the following year, and by the end of 2017, had received the Volunteer Award of the Year from the Sierra National Forest. The South Fork Trailblazers is now a flagship project of Mariposa Trails.



Our Vision

"All things in the world are two. In our minds, we are two- good and evil. With our eyes we see two things- things that are fair and things that are ugly. We have the right hand that strikes and makes evil and the left hand full of kindness near the heart. One foot may lead us to an evil way, the other foot may lead us to good. So are all things two, all two." -- Eagle Chief - Pawnee

In the context of land, water and sky, these words remind us of the duality of "taking" and "giving." As a “taker,” we’ve converted earth to commodities, and if one has already bought them, what more can be “given back?” Mariposa Trails recognizes the value-added gifts from nature and embraces the idea of "paying it forward," because health, prosperity and fun are grounded in our access to the earth, and our care of it.



Mission Statement

In partnership with public and private entities, Mariposa Trails works to create and maintain a meaningful trail network in Mariposa County. We do this through initiatives and programs that create space and activities where an intimate relationship between people and place can develop, benefiting the health of the individual, the community and the land.
